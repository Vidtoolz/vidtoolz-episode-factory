# Script: Stop Paying For AI Video — Your GPU Is Already a Studio

I want to tell you about the dumbest €47 I spent this year.

AI video credits. Kling. Runway. The usual suspects. Click generate, watch a progress bar, get a five-second clip of a robot walking through fog — then do it again. End of the month: forty-seven euros. For maybe thirty seconds of usable video.

Here's the thing. While I was paying Kling by the second, my own computer — with an RTX 4090 — was sitting in the other room doing absolutely nothing. It costs about fifteen cents an hour to run. And it can generate AI video. For free. Right now.

This isn't a hot take. This is math.

Making a one-minute video with background clips: generate ten clips, each needs two or three tries. That's twenty to thirty generations. At fifty cents per second, five seconds each — seventy-five dollars. Just for backgrounds. Just for one video.

Now the other side. Wan 2.2 — open-source AI video from Alibaba. Apache 2.0 license. Download it, run it on your own GPU. A five-second clip takes about three minutes on an RTX 4090. At fifteen cents an hour, that's one cent of electricity per clip. One cent versus two dollars and fifty cents.

Same quality. Different price. And bonus: privacy. Your prompts, your images — they never leave your computer. No company has your creative data.

I'm not pretending this is as easy as a web form. You need ComfyUI. You need to download models. You need to learn a node graph — which looks like a flowchart designed by someone who hates you. But once it's set up, it's yours.

And it's not just Wan. HunyuanVideo from Tencent. LTX-Video with native 4K and audio. Open weights. Free. The gap between open-source and commercial has basically closed. Commercial tools are selling convenience. Not better video.

If you have a gaming PC, you have an AI video studio. Not in the future. Right now. The models are free. The only thing you're paying for is not knowing that.

So here's my challenge. Go check your AI video subscriptions. Add up last month. Then look at your GPU. It might already be a better deal than whatever you're paying for.

And if your GPU can't run this yet — fair. But now you know the math. Next time someone tells you AI video is expensive, you'll know they're selling you something.
