## Final Edited Outline

### Thesis / angle

AI can help creators generate video ideas quickly, but a usable idea still needs a proof plan before it deserves scripting or shooting. A proof plan defines what the viewer will see, what example or demo will prove the promise, and what production evidence keeps the video from becoming generic advice.

### Opening hook

Before you ask AI to write a script, ask a harder question: what will this video prove on screen? A proof plan is the visible example, demo, comparison, or result that proves the video promise. If the proof is vague, the idea is not ready for production.

### Viewer problem

AI-assisted planning can make weak ideas look finished. A generated topic, title, outline, or hook may sound useful, but the final video still fails if there is no demo, no example, no before/after, and no clear viewer payoff.

### Promise setup

By the end of the video, viewers will have a simple proof-plan check they can apply before scripting or shooting an AI-assisted video idea.

### Section-by-section structure

1. **Why AI ideas often feel more complete than they are**
   - Show the prepared/stress-test idea: `10 AI Tools Every Creator Should Try in 2026`.
   - Explain why it sounds useful at first glance: clear topic, familiar list format, and obvious AI-era relevance.
   - Identify what is missing: specific viewer problem, proof moment, visuals, production path, and VIDTOOLZ point of view.
   - Principle: a convincing outline is not the same as a makeable video.

2. **The VIDTOOLZ proof-plan check**
   - Viewer promise: What will the viewer be able to do or understand by the end?
   - Proof moment: What specific demo, example, comparison, or result will prove that promise?
   - Visual evidence: What must appear on screen so the video is not only talking?
   - Production path: Can the proof be captured with available tools, time, and skill?
   - Final packaging fit: Will the title and thumbnail still match what the video actually proves?
   - Apply the check to the working example:
     - Weak promise: "here are 10 tools."
     - Stronger promise: "here is how to decide whether an AI tool deserves a place in your production workflow."
     - Proof moment: compare one shiny AI tool suggestion against a practical production need.
     - Visual evidence: tool list, decision table, keep/reject result.
     - Production path: screen recording plus a simple decision table.

3. **Before-and-after example**
   - Start with the vague prepared/stress-test idea: `10 AI Tools Every Creator Should Try in 2026`.
   - Mark the missing proof points.
   - Revise it into a stronger package:
     - Working title: `Stop Adding AI Tools Until They Pass This Workflow Test`
     - Viewer promise: viewers can judge whether an AI tool actually improves their video workflow.
     - Proof moment: one tool suggestion is tested against a real creator workflow need.
     - Visual plan: split screen of AI tool suggestion, decision table, and keep/reject result.
     - Production approach: talking-head setup plus screen recording and a simple proof-plan table.

4. **Where creator judgment beats automation**
   - AI can suggest topics, outline structures, and possible examples.
   - The creator decides whether the proof is real, useful, visible, and worth producing.
   - The goal is not a faster script. The goal is a video that can actually deliver.

### Key production principle

Do not judge an AI-assisted video idea only by how good it sounds. Judge it by what it can prove on screen.

### AI/tool evaluation moments

- Tool output should be pressure-tested against production reality.
