## Working Title

Stop Planning AI Videos Until You Have a Proof Plan

## Alternate repaired package title used inside the demo

Stop Adding AI Tools Until They Pass This Workflow Test

## Script

### Hook

Before you ask AI to write a script, ask one harder question:

What will this video prove on screen?

Because if the answer is vague, the idea is not ready for production yet.

A topic can sound good. A title can sound clickable. An outline can look finished. But none of that means the video has proof.

And for serious solo creators, that is where a lot of AI-assisted planning goes wrong.

### Setup

Here is the working example for this video.

Imagine AI gives you this idea:

`10 AI Tools Every Creator Should Try in 2026`

That sounds useful. It sounds current. It sounds like something a creator channel could make.

But before you script it, you need to ask what the video will actually prove.

Will it prove that these tools improve a real workflow?

Will it show a decision process?

Will it help the viewer choose, reject, or test a tool?

Or is it just a formatted list?

That difference matters.

### Promise

By the end of this video, you will have a simple proof-plan check you can use before scripting or shooting an AI-assisted video idea.

A proof plan is the visible example, demo, comparison, or result that proves the video promise.

If you cannot name the proof, the idea is probably not ready yet.

### Part 1: Why AI ideas can look more finished than they are

AI is good at making ideas look organized.

It can give you a title, an outline, a hook, a list of sections, and a clean structure very quickly. [VERIFY: exact tool behavior should be checked with the actual AI tool used on screen.]

But formatting is not the same as production value.

Take the idea:

`10 AI Tools Every Creator Should Try in 2026`

The surface is fine. The topic is relevant. The list format is familiar. The AI era angle is obvious.

But for VIDTOOLZ, it is still weak unless it answers a production question.

What viewer problem does it solve?

What proof will appear on screen?

What will be tested, compared, accepted, or rejected?

Why should this be a VIDTOOLZ video instead of a generic AI tools roundup?

If you cannot answer those questions, the idea is not a production plan. It is just a topic.

### Part 2: The proof-plan check

Here is the check I would run before writing the script.

First: viewer promise.

What will the viewer be able to do or understand by the end?

For the weak idea, the promise is basically:

Here are 10 tools.

That is not enough.

A stronger promise would be:

Here is how to decide whether an AI tool deserves a place in your production workflow.

Second: proof moment.

What specific demo, example, comparison, or result will prove that promise?

For this idea, the proof moment could be testing one shiny AI tool suggestion against a real creator workflow need.

Third: visual evidence.

What must appear on screen so the video is not just talking?

For this example, I would want to show the AI tool suggestion, a decision table, and a keep-or-reject result.

Fourth: production path.

Can I capture the proof with the tools, time, and skill I actually have?

For this version, the production path is simple enough: screen recording plus a decision table.

Fifth: final packaging fit.

Will the title and thumbnail still match what the video proves?

If the video is not really about 10 tools, the title should not promise 10 tools.

### Part 3: Repair the weak idea

So instead of making:

`10 AI Tools Every Creator Should Try in 2026`

I would repair the package into something like:

`Stop Adding AI Tools Until They Pass This Workflow Test`

That is a stronger VIDTOOLZ angle because it is not just about tools.

It is about judgment.

The viewer promise becomes:

By the end, you will know how to decide whether an AI tool actually improves your video workflow.

The proof moment becomes:

Take one tool suggestion and test it against a real production need.

The visual plan becomes:

AI suggestion on one side, decision table in the middle, keep-or-reject result at the end.

The production approach becomes:

Talking-head setup, screen recording, and one simple proof-plan table.

That is much more makeable.

It gives the video something to prove.

### Part 4: Where creator judgment beats automation

This is the point.

AI can suggest topics. It can give you possible structures. It can even help pressure-test a package.

But it cannot decide whether your video has enough real proof to deserve production time.

That is the creator's job.

Your job is to ask:

What will the viewer see?

What will the example prove?

What changes between the weak version and the stronger version?

Can I actually capture this?

Does the final title and thumbnail match the proof?

If those answers are weak, do not move straight into scripting.

Repair the idea first.

### Recap

A good AI-assisted video idea is not just a topic, title, or outline.

It needs a proof plan.

For this example, `10 AI Tools Every Creator Should Try in 2026` sounds useful, but it becomes stronger when it changes into:

`Stop Adding AI Tools Until They Pass This Workflow Test`

Now the video has a viewer promise, a proof moment, visual evidence, and a production path.

That is the difference between an AI-generated idea and a video worth making.

### CTA

Before you script your next AI-assisted video idea, write one sentence:

This video will prove _____ by showing _____.

If you cannot fill in that sentence clearly, revise the idea before you shoot.
