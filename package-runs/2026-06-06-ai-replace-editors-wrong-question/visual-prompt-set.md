## Concept #1 — Split Screen: AI-Cut Cold vs Human-Cut Warm

**Use:** Hook + Claim. "AI-cut sequence, mechanical, rhythmically dead" vs "human pacing, intentional holds, breather moments."

**Image prompt:**

Vertical composition, split exactly down the middle. LEFT HALF: a video editing timeline in cold blue monochrome. Cuts are placed at mechanically even intervals — sterile, lifeless, identical spacing. No variation. The tracks are flat. The color is clinical blue-gray. The feel is dead, automated, wrong.

RIGHT HALF: the same timeline in warm amber and gold tones. Cuts land with intentional rhythm — some held long, some quick. The spacing breathes like music. One cut is held deliberately longer than the others, a held moment glowing slightly. The tracks have life. The color is warm, human, intentional.

The dividing line between the halves is sharp — no gradient, no blend. Cold vs warm. Dead vs alive.

Style: Clean, modern, interface-inspired. No text labels. The difference must read purely through color, rhythm, and spacing.

**Wan A14B motion hint:** The right side (warm) should have a subtle breathing quality — cuts appearing with slight organic variation. The left side (cold) cuts appear at identical intervals, mechanical.

## Concept #2 — Audio Waveform Detail with Held Silence

**Use:** Example. "The held silence — two extra seconds of reaction instead of cutting to the quote."

**Image prompt:**

Extreme close-up of an audio waveform on a professional studio monitor. Two dialogue tracks visible vertically. Top track: the reporter's voice, waveform active but fading. Bottom track: the interviewee's quote, waveform about to begin. Between them — a gap of silence. That gap is highlighted with a warm golden glow, as if the editor's cursor is resting there, deliberate.

The waveform detail is crisp and technical — you can see the individual peaks and valleys. The color palette is dark studio tones: charcoal blacks, soft monitor glow, the warm amber of waveform highlights. No faces, no people. Just the waveform and the held silence.

The composition says: this moment matters. This gap is the editing.

Style: Photorealistic, technical, intimate. Shallow depth of field on the waveform.

**Wan A14B motion hint:** The golden glow on the silence gap breathes subtly — a slow, meditative pulse. The waveforms themselves remain still or drift microscopically.

## Concept #3 — Clock / Edit-Marker Meditation

**Use:** Reframe. "The hands don't race — they're deliberate. Warm light cycles from day to night."

**Image prompt:**

A large clock face filling the frame, but it's not a normal clock. The hands of the clock are video-editing markers — timeline markers, in and out points, glowing amber against the dark clock face. Instead of numbers 1 through 12, the face has subtle editing symbols: a blade icon, a playhead triangle, a waveform squiggle.

The hands are not racing. They're moving with deliberate, intentional slowness — like someone thinking, not someone panicking. Behind the clock, the ambient light cycles from warm daylight gold (left edge) to deep edit-suite night blue (right edge) — the long edit session, the late hours.

No people. No text. Pure metaphor.

Style: Cinematic, warm, slightly abstract but readable. The clock should feel like an editor's mind, not a deadline.

**Wan A14B motion hint:** The hands move very slowly — deliberate. The ambient light behind the clock cycles subtly between warm day and cool night.

## Concept #4 — Evolution Montage: Razor → Avid → NLE → AI

**Use:** Setup. "I've seen this movie before."

**Image prompt:**

A horizontal sequence of four editing eras, arranged left to right across the vertical frame, each dissolving into the next. Far left: a film razor blade and tape splicer on a light table, warm amber film-stock glow, slightly dusty. Next: a Steenbeck flatbed editor, reels turning, mechanical and solid. Next: a CRT monitor showing a generic early digital editing interface, green phosphor tinge — the idea of an NLE, not a specific product. Far right: a modern ultrawide monitor with a sleek generic editing timeline, cool digital precision.

Each era fades into the next through a visible dissolve — not a hard cut. The color palette shifts with each era: warm amber → tungsten → green phosphor → cool digital blue. The overall composition feels like looking at editing history in one breath.

IMPORTANT: No branded UI fidelity required. The CRT and modern monitor interfaces should be recognizable as editing tools but remain generic — shapes, tracks, waveforms — without depending on pixel-perfect reproduction of any specific software. The machines and their era-appropriate glow carry the meaning.

Style: Cinematic, slightly nostalgic, documentary feel. No people. The machines are the characters.

**Wan A14B motion hint:** Slow, continuous dissolve from left to right. Each era breathes for a moment before fading into the next. A single smooth evolution shot.

**Wan A14B motion hint:** Subtle. The timeline glow has a gentle pulse. The chair remains still and solid. A slight atmospheric haze drifts.

**Image prompt:**

Style: Cinematic silhouette photography. Warm backlight, slight haze, epic but intimate.

**Wan A14B motion hint:** The tools orbit slowly around the silhouette. New tools fade into the orbit gently. The overall motion is calm, meditative, almost planetary. The silhouette remains still.

## Visual Style Consistency

All 6 images share a unified visual language:

- **Mood:** Cinematic editorial — warm, grounded, human. Nothing sterile, nothing corporate, nothing sci-fi.
- **Composition:** Clean, intentional, readable. Each frame has one clear subject. No visual clutter.
- **Atmosphere:** Believable professional environment — edit suites, studios, tool benches. Familiar to someone who's done the work.
- **Color palette:** Warm amber and gold tones dominant. Deep edit-suite blacks. Occasional cool blue/green as counterpoint (Concept #1 split, #4 evolution). No neon, no harsh white.
- **Text/UI rule:** No cluttered fake UI text anywhere. Interface elements (timelines, waveforms, monitors) are atmospheric and shape-based, not pixel-accurate reproductions. The idea of the tool, not the screenshot of it.
- **People:** None visible. If a silhouette appears (#6), it's abstract, backlit, universal — not a specific person.
- **Motion intent (for Wan):** All motion is subtle, deliberate, breathing — never frantic, never flashy.
