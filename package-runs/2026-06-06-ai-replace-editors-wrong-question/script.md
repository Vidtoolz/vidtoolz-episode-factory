## Hook

People keep asking me if I'm worried AI is going to replace video editors.

And I get it. Every time a new tool drops, someone writes the headline. "AI just edited a whole video." "This model cuts faster than a human." "The editor is dead."

I've been hearing this for twenty years. And I'm still here.

## Setup — I've seen this movie before

Here's the thing. I started editing on Avid at YLE when tape was still a thing. I've been through the razor blade, the tape-to-tape, the NLE transition, the digital wave, the cloud. Every single time, someone said the editor was dead.

And every single time, the job didn't disappear. It expanded.

[Visual: Evolution montage — razor blade and tape splicer → Steenbeck flatbed → CRT with Avid → modern NLE. Each phase fades into the next. Concept #4 — Evolution montage: razor → Avid → NLE → AI.]

When NLEs showed up, people said "now anyone can cut." And yeah — anyone can cut. But not everyone can edit. There's a difference.

---

## The claim — editing isn't shrinking, it's expanding

The mistake people make is thinking editing is just about cutting clips together. That's like saying cooking is just about chopping vegetables.

Real editing — the thing you get paid for — is judgment. It's knowing when to hold a beat and when to cut it. It's understanding what a director means, not what they say. It's feeling the rhythm of a scene and knowing it's off by four frames.

AI cannot do any of that. Not because the models are bad — because those things aren't data problems. They're human problems.

[Visual: Split screen — left side: AI-cut sequence, mechanical, rhythmically dead. Right side: same sequence with human pacing, intentional holds, breather moments. Concept #1 — Split screen: AI-cut cold vs human-cut warm.]

## The concrete example — one real news-editing moment

Let me give you a real example. This one's from current affairs.

I'm cutting a reporter package. Two-minute piece, tight deadline — the usual. The reporter's done a solid interview, but there's one quote that's... fine. Not great. Not terrible. Just... fine.

Now, the obvious edit is to cut to the quote, let it play, move on. That's what an auto-cut would do. It sees a soundbite. It cuts to it.

But I know the story. I know the piece is dragging here. So instead of cutting to the quote, I hold on the reporter's face — just listening. Two seconds. Then I bring the quote in under her reaction. The quote becomes narration. The story breathes.

That decision — to not cut when the timeline says cut — that's editing. No prompt can write that. No model can feel that.

And if you've ever sat in an edit suite and felt it click, you know what I mean. The machine says one thing. Your gut says another. And your gut wins.

[Visual: Close-up of audio waveform on a studio monitor. Two dialogue tracks visible — the space between them highlighted, glowing. The held silence. Concept #2 — Audio waveform detail with held silence.]

## The reframe — direct, don't compete

So the question isn't "will AI replace editors?"

The real question is: can you learn to use AI the way you learned to use every tool before it?

Because here's what I've learned: the editor who treats AI as a threat loses. The editor who treats it as another tool — the way they treated the Avid, the NLE, the grade, the mix — that editor is going to be fine.

You're not competing with a machine at cutting. You're directing it. Same as you'd direct an assistant editor. You make the calls. It does the prep.

[Visual: Clock face with edit markers as hands. Time moves forward, cuts appear and disappear, the hands don't race — they're deliberate. Warm light cycles from day to night. Concept #3 — Clock/edit-marker meditation.]

## Closing — the real threat isn't AI

The editors who should be worried aren't the ones who can direct AI. They're the ones who refuse to learn it.

Same as the editors who refused to learn NLEs in the 90s. Same as the ones who insisted tape would never die.

The pattern is always the same. The tool changes. The craft doesn't.

[Visual: Director's chair — the classic canvas kind — sitting behind a glowing timeline interface. The chair back says "EDITOR." Concept #5 — Director's chair behind timeline.]

## CTA

Stop asking if AI will replace you.
Start asking what kind of editor you want to be when the tools change.

Because the tools always change. They always have. And the editors who are still here — we didn't survive by fighting the new thing.

We survived by learning it.
